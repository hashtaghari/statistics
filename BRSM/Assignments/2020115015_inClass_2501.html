for sz in [50,100,500,1500]:
    means = []
    std_dvs =[]
    
    for i in range(1,1001):
        # Generate a set of random numbers from a normal distribution
        data = np.random.normal(mean, std_dev, 10) 

        # calculate mean and std dev of data
        mean_data = np.mean(data)
        std_dev_data = np.std(data)
        # print(mean_data, std_dev_data)
        means.append(mean_data)
        std_dvs.append(std_dev_data)

    # Plot the data
    plt.hist(means, bins=50)
    plt.xlabel('Mean')
    plt.ylabel('Frequency')
    plt.title(f'Mean of {sz} random numbers from the Normal Distribution N(100,15)')
    plt.show()

    # Plot the data
    plt.hist(std_dvs, bins=50)
    plt.xlabel('Std Dev')
    plt.ylabel('Frequency')
    plt.title(f'Std Dev of {sz} random numbers from the Normal Distribution N(100,15)')
    plt.show()

The above histograms are clear examples that depict the central limit theorem. We can see that drawing random samples from a distribution (N(100,15)) in this case, leads to a normal distribution of means of the datapoints obtained.

We also observe in all the above graphs that the distribution of means and the standard deviation revolves around 100 and 15 resspectively, imitating the original distribution. As the sample of datapoints drawn increses, the graphs seem to resemble more and more of how a Normal distribution with mean 100 and standard deviation of 15 would look like, hence mimicing the original dataset in this case. We also observe that the distribution of means gets tighter with increasing sample size.

for sz in [30, 70, 100]:
    a = np.random.normal(0, 1, sz)
    b = np.random.normal(2, 0.5, sz)
    c = np.random.normal(3, 2, sz)
    # calculate mean and std dev of data
    mean_a = np.mean(a)
    std_dev_a = np.std(a)
    mean_b = np.mean(b)
    std_dev_b = np.std(b)
    mean_c = np.mean(c)
    std_dev_c = np.std(c)
    # plot the mean with error bars for each group 
    plt.errorbar(['a', 'b', 'c'], [mean_a, mean_b, mean_c], yerr=[std_dev_a, std_dev_b, std_dev_c], fmt='o')
    plt.xlabel('Group')
    plt.ylabel('Mean')
    plt.title(f'Group means with error bars for sample size {sz}')
    plt.show()

Theoritically, the confidence interval should get smaller with increase in sample size as we will become more precise in estimating the meme. However, in this case we to observe that the confidence interval did not change much. Although, in all of the sample sizes, the mean of the samples for each group revolved very close to the actual population mean.
